AHA vs BHA: Understanding the Difference

AHAs (Alpha Hydroxy Acids) are water-soluble acids that work on the skin surface. They dissolve the bonds between dead skin cells, accelerating their removal and revealing fresher, smoother skin underneath. The most common AHAs are glycolic acid (from sugar cane) and lactic acid (from milk). AHAs are best for sun damage, fine lines, uneven texture, and dull skin that needs brightening.

BHAs (Beta Hydroxy Acids) are oil-soluble, meaning they can penetrate into pores and dissolve the sebum and dead skin cells that cause breakouts. Salicylic acid is the primary BHA used in skincare. It also has anti-inflammatory properties that reduce redness associated with acne. BHAs are best for oily, acne-prone skin, blackheads, and enlarged pores.

PHAs (Polyhydroxy Acids) are the gentlest chemical exfoliants, suitable for sensitive skin that cannot tolerate AHAs or BHAs. Gluconolactone and lactobionic acid are common PHAs. Their larger molecular size means slower, more gradual exfoliation with less irritation. PHAs also function as humectants, attracting moisture to the skin while exfoliating. They are the best starting point for exfoliation-naive skin.

How to Use Chemical Exfoliants Safely

Start slowly regardless of which exfoliant you choose. Begin with once or twice per week, applying in the evening after cleansing. If your skin tolerates the product without excessive dryness, redness, or stinging after two weeks, increase to every other night. Some people build up to nightly use while others do best with two to three times per week long-term.

Apply chemical exfoliants to clean, dry skin. Wait 5-10 minutes after application before layering other products to allow the acid to work at its intended pH level. Applying moisturizer immediately can buffer the exfoliant and reduce its effectiveness. After the waiting period, proceed with the rest of your evening routine including serums and moisturizer.

Never combine multiple exfoliating products in the same routine. Using an AHA toner followed by a BHA serum in the same evening is a recipe for barrier damage, irritation, and sensitization. If you want to use both AHA and BHA, alternate nights rather than layering them. On retinol nights, skip all exfoliants. Over-exfoliation is one of the most common causes of skin barrier damage.

Signs of Over-Exfoliation and How to Recover

Over-exfoliated skin shows characteristic signs: persistent redness, stinging when applying even gentle products, increased sensitivity to products that previously caused no reaction, a shiny but tight appearance, increased breakouts, and skin that feels dry despite moisturizing. If you recognize multiple signs, you have damaged your skin barrier through excessive exfoliation.

Recovery requires immediately stopping all exfoliating products and simplifying your routine to just a gentle cleanser, a barrier-repair moisturizer with ceramides, and sunscreen. Your damaged barrier is more susceptible to UV damage during recovery. Products containing centella asiatica, ceramides, niacinamide, and hyaluronic acid actively support barrier repair.

Full recovery from over-exfoliation typically takes 2-6 weeks depending on the severity. Do not resume exfoliation until your skin feels completely normal with no sensitivity. When reintroducing exfoliants, start at the lowest frequency (once weekly) with the gentlest product available. The goal is consistent gentle exfoliation that supports natural skin turnover without compromising barrier integrity.

Choosing the Right Exfoliant for Your Concerns

For acne and blackheads: BHA (salicylic acid) is the clear choice. Its oil-soluble nature allows it to penetrate pores and dissolve the sebum plugs that cause blackheads and breakouts. The anti-inflammatory properties also calm existing acne. Paula Choice 2% BHA or CosRx BHA Blackhead Power Liquid are the top recommendations.

For dullness and uneven tone: AHA (glycolic or lactic acid) accelerates cell turnover, revealing brighter skin underneath. Glycolic acid is the most potent AHA for dramatic results. Lactic acid is gentler and also provides humectant moisture benefits, making it ideal for dry or sensitive skin that needs brightening without aggravation.

For aging and fine lines: AHA works best for surface-level concerns like fine lines and sun damage spots. Glycolic acid at 5-10% concentration used consistently improves skin texture and reduces the appearance of fine lines over 8-12 weeks. Combine with retinol on alternate nights for a comprehensive anti-aging strategy that addresses both surface and deeper skin layers.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I use AHA and BHA together?

Using AHA and BHA in the same routine increases irritation risk. Alternate them on different nights instead. Use BHA on nights when your pores need attention and AHA when you want to brighten and smooth. Some combination products are formulated to use both safely, but proceed with caution.

How often should I exfoliate my face?

Start with once or twice per week and increase based on your skin tolerance. Most people do well with 2-3 times per week. Daily exfoliation is possible with gentle formulations but unnecessary for most skin types. Over-exfoliation damages the skin barrier and causes more problems than it solves.

Should I exfoliate before or after cleansing?

After cleansing. Clean skin allows the exfoliant to work directly on the skin surface without dirt and oil interfering. Apply to clean, dry skin, wait 5-10 minutes, then continue with the rest of your routine. Never apply chemical exfoliants to wet skin as water can increase penetration and irritation.
